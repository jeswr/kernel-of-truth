# Fixed vector representations inside language models: the evidence

**Kernel of Truth programme — web-research stream (falsification-oriented)**
Date: 2026-07-07. Author: research agent. Coordination: sparq-org/sparq#1683.

**Question.** Does the literature support building a "kernel" of formalised concepts with canonical, **untrained, frozen, externally-specified** vector representations into LLM/LCM architectures, with the claimed benefits of (1) guaranteed understanding of kernel concepts, (2) training savings, (3) more efficient dense concept-level I/O, (4) eventual GNN-style concept I/O and internal rules-based inference?

**Method note.** Evidence gathered by web search over arXiv/ACL/OpenReview/venue pages, July 2026. Each finding is tagged **[established]** (peer-reviewed and/or replicated, load-bearing numbers verified), **[claimed]** (published but single-source, small-scale, or with caveats that weaken the headline), or **[speculative]** (preprint/position/theory without empirical validation). This stream was explicitly tasked with finding disconfirming evidence; the verdict section weighs both directions.

---

## 1. Frozen / fixed input embeddings

**The input embedding of every transformer is already a static, per-type vector at inference time.** [established — architectural fact] Contextualisation happens strictly *after* the embedding lookup. This means the kernel proposal's input-side mechanics are not exotic; what is novel is (a) freezing during training and (b) external specification of the values.

**Models train fine around frozen — even meaningless — embeddings.**

- *Reservoir Transformers* (Shen, Baevski, Morcos, Keutzer, Auli, Kiela; ACL 2021, arXiv:2012.15045) [established]: interleaving **frozen randomly-initialised** transformer layers with trainable ones matches or improves MT and (masked) LM quality per wall-clock. Builds on Wieting & Kiela (ICLR 2019, arXiv:1901.10444) [established]: random sentence encoders are a strong baseline for classification.
- *Pretrained Transformers as Universal Computation Engines* / Frozen Pretrained Transformer (Lu, Grover, Abbeel, Mordatch; AAAI 2022, arXiv:2103.05247) [established]: freeze all attention/FFN blocks, train only input/output projections (+ layernorm), and the model transfers across modalities competitively. The inverse of the kernel setup — trainable periphery, frozen core — but it demonstrates the same underlying fact: transformers are highly tolerant of large frozen subsets, with a thin trained adapter absorbing the mismatch.
- *Emergent Semantics Beyond Token Embeddings* (arXiv:2507.04886, TMLR 2025) [claimed]: GPT-style decoders (0.2–0.5B params, ~9B tokens) trained with the **entire embedding layer frozen to non-semantic vectors derived from Unicode glyph visuals** converge and generate coherent text. Headline claim that frozen models "beat trainable embeddings on MMLU" should be heavily discounted: the reported scores (22.29 vs 11.37 at 0.5B) are at or **below the 25% random-chance floor of MMLU**, so the comparison is noise around chance, not evidence of a reasoning benefit. The robust and useful finding is the control: with embeddings frozen to **random pixel noise**, models still converge but **5–10× slower**. Interpretation offered by the authors — semantic structure in embeddings is an *emergent artifact*, not a prerequisite; the network learns its own interpretation function around whatever fixed symbols it is given.
- *Attention Retrieves, MLP Memorizes* (arXiv:2506.01115) [claimed]: component-freezing ablations further support that much of the transformer's function is carried by specific trainable components, not the embedding table.

**Does the embedding layer matter much?**

- *Rethinking Embedding Coupling* (Chung, Fevry, Tsai, Johnson, Ruder; ICLR 2021, arXiv:2010.12821) [established]: the **input** embedding dimension can be shrunk substantially without hurting downstream performance; capacity matters at the **output** embedding. ALBERT (ICLR 2020) reached a similar conclusion via factorised embeddings. The input embedding is one of the *least* performance-critical parameter blocks in a large model.
- *Tied input/output embeddings* (Press & Wolf, EACL 2017, arXiv:1608.05859) [established]: tying improves perplexity in RNN-era and small-transformer LMs and became the default; but Chung et al. (above) and *In-Domain Embedding Initialisation* (arXiv:2009.14109, EMNLP 2020) [established] show tying is **not** beneficial in all regimes (decoupling wins for multilingual and for in-domain-initialised embeddings), and recent work (*Weight Tying Biases Token Embeddings Towards the Output Space*, arXiv:2603.26663, 2026 preprint) [claimed] shows tying warps input embeddings toward output-space geometry. Kernel-relevant: if a kernel vector had to serve as both input symbol and output target, likelihood training is known to exert degenerative pressure on output embeddings (Gao et al., *Representation Degeneration*, ICLR 2019, arXiv:1907.12009) [established]. A frozen kernel escapes the degeneration but then the rest of the network must compensate.
- *In-domain init + freezing* (arXiv:2009.14109) [established, low-resource regime]: initialising input embeddings from in-domain word2vec **and freezing them** helps LM quality at 10–100M-token scale, chiefly via rare words. No evidence this survives at web scale, where rare-word statistics are learned anyway.

**Bottom line for §1.** Freezing embeddings is *survivable* and sometimes marginally helpful; what is frozen barely matters at convergence (random works; glyph pixels work), because downstream layers learn to treat the vectors as arbitrary identifiers. This cuts **against** the kernel's accuracy claim: a canonical semantic geometry at the input is neither required nor demonstrably *used as semantics* by the model — and cuts only weakly **for** the training-efficiency claim, since the parameters saved are among the least important in the model, and non-adapted initialisations *slow* convergence (5–10× for random noise).

## 2. Externally-imposed semantic structure at the input layer

- *Retrofitting* (Faruqui et al., NAACL 2015, arXiv:1411.4166) [established]: post-hoc graph-based adjustment of word vectors toward WordNet/PPDB/FrameNet improves **word-similarity and some semantic tasks**. Crucially, the literature that followed measured mostly *intrinsic* benchmarks; evidence that retrofitted inputs improve *language-model quality* (perplexity, generation, reasoning) is thin to absent. This is exactly the gap the kernel hypothesis sits in.
- *E-BERT* (Poerner, Waltinger, Schütze; Findings of EMNLP 2020, arXiv:1911.03681) [established]: the closest existing thing to a working "kernel plugin". Wikipedia2Vec entity vectors are mapped by a **single learned linear transformation** into BERT's wordpiece embedding space and used **as if they were wordpiece vectors**, with no further pretraining. Beats BERT and matches/beats ERNIE/KnowBERT on unsupervised QA (LAMA), relation classification, entity linking, and fixes a real failure mode (surface-form guessing: "Italian-sounding name ⇒ speaks Italian"). Key nuances: the host model was already trained; the entity vectors were *distributionally* trained (not formal/canonical); and a learned aligner was still required.
- *KG-enhanced pretrained LMs* — ERNIE (ACL 2019, arXiv:1905.07129), KnowBERT (EMNLP 2019, arXiv:1909.04164), KEPLER (TACL 2021, arXiv:1911.06136) [established, mixed]: injecting KG entity embeddings helps entity-centric tasks, but the KEPLER evaluation found ERNIE and KnowBERT **degrade in most out-of-domain settings** (FewRel 2.0), because they are welded to a specific entity linker and a specific frozen entity-embedding set. The Survey of Knowledge-Enhanced PLMs (arXiv:2110.00269) [established] records that this entire research line delivered narrow, task-local gains, did not move general LM quality, and was effectively abandoned once plain scaling and RAG outperformed it. This is a direct historical precedent for "external canonical vectors welded into an LM": it worked *locally*, was *brittle globally*, and *lost to scaling*.
- *MoSECroT* (arXiv:2401.04821) [claimed]: static word embeddings + model stitching for cross-lingual zero-shot transfer — feasible but weak versus trained baselines.

**Bottom line for §2.** Externally computed vectors *can* be injected into a trained LM through a cheap learned aligner and help on knowledge-intensive tasks (E-BERT). Every attempt to make external semantic structure a *load-bearing, general* part of an LM has been brittle out of domain and superseded by scaling. Nothing in this literature tests truly *formal* (non-distributional) canonical vectors as inputs — that specific cell of the matrix is empty. The kernel hypothesis is here **untested rather than supported**.

## 3. Cross-model representation convergence — does a canonical geometry exist?

- *Relative representations* (Moschella et al., ICLR 2023 oral, arXiv:2209.15430) [established]: representing each sample by its similarities to a fixed set of **anchor samples** yields coordinates invariant to rotations/rescalings of the latent space, enabling **zero-shot stitching** of encoders and decoders trained separately, across seeds, architectures and modalities. This is the strongest evidence that a *shared, model-independent coordinate system* is achievable — but note its shape: the canonical coordinates are defined **relative to data anchors, computed inside each model's own learned space**. Nothing is externally specified or untrained; the invariance is *discovered*, not imposed.
- *Model stitching* (Lenc & Vedaldi, CVPR 2015, arXiv:1411.5908; Bansal, Nakkiran, Barak, NeurIPS 2021, arXiv:2106.07682) [established]: well-trained networks of the same architecture, and even different training regimes, can be stitched with **a trained affine layer** at minimal accuracy cost ("stitching connectivity"). The affine layer is not optional — raw activations are *not* interchangeable.
- *Platonic Representation Hypothesis* (Huh, Cheung, Isola et al., ICML 2024 position, arXiv:2405.07987) [claimed — position paper with supporting measurements]: as models scale, their representational kernels (pairwise-distance structure) converge across modalities, plausibly toward a shared statistical model of reality.
- *vec2vec* (Jha, Zhang, Shmatikov, Morris, arXiv:2505.12540; and mini-vec2vec, arXiv:2510.02348) [claimed, replicated once]: text embeddings from unrelated encoders can be translated into one another **without any paired data**, via a learned map through a shared latent space — cosine up to 0.92, top-1 up to 100% on some pairs. Strong evidence the *geometry* is convergent; the translator is nonetheless a **trained** network (mini-vec2vec shows near-linear suffices).
- Counter-evidence on raw-vector portability: *soft prompt transferability* (Su et al., NAACL 2022, arXiv:2111.06719; PromptBridge, arXiv:2512.01420) [established]: continuous prompt vectors tuned for one model **do not transfer** to another model directly; even with a trained cross-model projector they lose ~10.9–15.7 points of absolute accuracy moving between GPT-2 and OPT. Vectors that mean something to one network are gibberish to another until re-mapped.

**Bottom line for §3.** Convergence is real **up to a learned transformation**. This supports a *weak* kernel: one canonical concept geometry, plus a small trained adapter per model, could interoperate with many models — that is essentially relative representations / vec2vec territory. It **undercuts** the *strong* kernel: no result shows an externally fixed coordinate system working across (or even within) models with **zero** trained interface. And in every convergence result, the shared geometry is the *product of training on data* — the convergent geometry is discovered by learning, which is precisely the resource the kernel proposes to skip.

## 4. Concept-level models — the dense-I/O claim meets measurement

- *SONAR* (Duquenne et al. 2023, arXiv:2308.11466) [established]: fixed-size multilingual sentence-embedding space, 200 languages, trained on translation objectives; strong on xsim/xsim++ retrieval.
- *Large Concept Models* (Meta FAIR, Dec 2024, arXiv:2412.08821) [established paper, claims mixed]: autoregressive next-**sentence**-embedding prediction in frozen SONAR space; MSE-regression, diffusion, and quantised variants; 1.6B (1.3T tokens) → 7B (2.7T tokens). Honest reading of results: good **zero-shot cross-lingual transfer** on summarisation (the headline win, inherited from SONAR's multilinguality); on core English generative quality it did **not** surpass same-size token LLMs, and the paper itself documents the structural problems: (a) **fragility of the frozen embedding space** — long/complex sentences (>~250 chars) degrade encode–decode quality so much they capped segmentation at 200 chars; (b) sentence segmentation is a load-bearing, unsolved preprocessing step; (c) valid sentences form "a cloud of points rather than a real continuous distribution" in SONAR space — the model must hit a sparse manifold for the decoder to produce fluent text.
- *SONAR-LLM* (arXiv:2508.05305) [claimed]: the cleanest quantitative comparison found. Scaling exponents on validation loss: **token-level LLM α ≈ 0.791** vs SONAR-LLM ≈ 0.569, MSE-LCM ≈ 0.515, diffusion-LCM ≈ 0.485. Every concept-space variant **scales worse than token prediction**; concept-space decoding buys fewer decode steps, at a scaling-law penalty. Follow-up activity on LCM at Meta since Dec 2024 is limited (community follow-ons like SONAR-LLM and surveys, arXiv:2501.05487); no scaled LCM-2 has appeared as of July 2026 — weak but real revealed-preference evidence.
- *Byte Latent Transformer* (Meta, arXiv:2412.09871) [established]: tokenizer-free, byte-level model whose latent "patches" **match token-LLM performance at 8B scale with better inference FLOPs and robustness**. Directionally important for the kernel debate: BLT succeeds by making the latent units **learned, dynamic, entropy-driven, and content-agnostic** — the exact opposite of fixed, semantic, externally specified units. (Also MEGABYTE, arXiv:2305.07185.)
- *Coconut — latent chain-of-thought* (Hao et al., arXiv:2412.06769) [claimed]: feeding hidden states back as "continuous thoughts" beats CoT on synthetic planning benchmarks (ProntoQA/ProsQA) with fewer tokens; but *Do Latent Tokens Think?* (arXiv:2512.21711) [claimed] finds causal/adversarial evidence that latent tokens are often not faithful reasoning carriers. Latent-space "concept" computation trades away inspectability, which is the opposite of the kernel's accuracy story.
- *Backpack LMs* (Hewitt, Thickstun, Manning, Liang; ACL 2023, arXiv:2305.16765) [established]: a 170M Backpack matches GPT-2-small loss while routing all predictions through **non-contextual, interpretable sense vectors** that support predictable intervention (debiasing, topic control). The best existing evidence that *architecturally privileged static semantic vectors* can coexist with competitive LM quality — but the sense vectors are **learned**, not externally specified, and the demonstration is at 2019-scale.

**Bottom line for §4.** Concept-level I/O has been built and measured. Where it wins (multilinguality, decode-step count, controllability) the wins are real; but on the core question — does dense semantic I/O beat token I/O on general quality per compute — the measured answer so far is **no** (scaling exponents, SONAR fragility, no scaled follow-up). Fixed *semantic* unit spaces have been outperformed by learned *non-semantic* unit spaces (BLT).

## 5. Vocabulary extension and hand-set embeddings — the micro-version of the kernel

Adding a new token with an externally computed embedding is precisely "one kernel concept, one vector". The literature is well developed:

- Hewitt, *Initializing New Word Embeddings for Pretrained LMs* (2021, columbia.edu/~johnhew) [established]: naive (e.g., zero or far-out-of-distribution) init of new tokens breaks the model — mean-of-existing-embeddings + noise bounds the KL between pre- and post-expansion LMs. New vectors must live **inside the model's existing embedding distribution** to be usable at all.
- WECHSEL (NAACL 2022, arXiv:2112.06598), FOCUS (arXiv:2305.14481), ZeTT (Minixhofer et al., NeurIPS 2024, arXiv:2405.07883) [established]: semantically informed initialisation of new-token embeddings accelerates adaptation — i.e., *good external semantics helps as an initialiser*.
- *An Empirical Comparison of Vocabulary Expansion and Initialization Approaches* (arXiv:2407.05841) [established]: after modest continued pretraining, **simple multivariate/random-within-convex-hull initialisation matches the sophisticated semantic methods**. External semantic content buys a head start, not an endpoint; the model overwrites/re-purposes it. Directly deflationary for "canonical vectors as permanent ground truth".
- Soft prompts (Lester, Al-Rfou, Constant, EMNLP 2021, arXiv:2104.08691) [established]: a handful of **continuous input vectors** can steer a frozen LLM to match full fine-tuning at scale — proof that input-layer vectors are a high-bandwidth control channel. But they are (a) *learned against the specific frozen model*, and (b) non-transferable across models (§3). No literature was found on *hand-set, never-trained* soft prompts performing usefully.
- xRAG (arXiv:2405.13792, NeurIPS 2024) [established] and KBLaM (Microsoft, ICLR 2025, arXiv:2410.10450) [established]: retrieval/document/KB content injected as **dense vectors** (one token per document via a 2-layer MLP bridge; KB triples as key-value pairs via linear adapters + rectangular attention). Both work and are efficient (xRAG: 3.53× FLOP reduction, +10% avg over compression baselines; KBLaM: 10K+ triples in an 8K-context 8B model, learns to refuse when the KB lacks the answer). Both also quantify the cost: xRAG retains roughly **62–73%** of the capability of giving the model the actual text. Dense vector input is real, useful, and **lossy**, and always mediated by a **trained** projector.

**Bottom line for §5.** The micro-experiments most analogous to the kernel all agree: externally computed vectors work as *initialisers* or as *adapter-mediated inputs to trained models*; they must lie in the model's own distribution; their semantic content is either retrained away or interpreted through a trained bridge; and hand-set-and-frozen vectors with **no** trained interface have no supporting results at all.

## 6. The contextualisation objection, given precise shape

The objection is not "static vectors can't feed contextual models" — they already do, in every transformer. The precise questions are:

1. **Does the model treat the static input vector as a *semantic* point?** Mechanistic evidence says mostly no. *Stages of Inference* (Lad, Gurnee, Tegmark, arXiv:2406.19384, NeurIPS 2025) [established]: the first layers perform **detokenization** — aggressively aggregating and re-representing raw token vectors into entity/concept representations; these early layers are among the *least* skippable in the network. The input embedding is raw material, not meaning. *Weight-based detokenization analysis* (arXiv:2501.15754) [claimed] concurs. ROME (Meng et al., NeurIPS 2022, arXiv:2202.05262) [established]: factual associations are stored and retrievable in **mid-layer MLPs** acting as key-value memories — editing a fact means editing MLPs, not embeddings. What the model "believes" about a concept therefore lives outside any input vector: **fixing the vector does not fix the interpretation.**
2. **Is the embedding geometry the model wants compatible with a clean semantic geometry?** Trained embedding spaces are strongly **anisotropic** — a narrow cone, worse in later layers (Ethayarajh, EMNLP 2019, arXiv:1909.00512) [established]; representation degeneration under tied likelihood training (Gao et al., ICLR 2019) [established]; a few rogue dimensions dominate similarity (Timkey & van Schijndel, EMNLP 2021) [established]; though isotropy re-emerges within clusters (Cai et al., ICLR 2021) [claimed]. A canonical kernel geometry (presumably isotropic, semantically calibrated) is *not* the geometry trained models converge to; imposing it means either the model warps around it (feasible per §1, at a convergence cost) or an adapter reconciles the two (a trained component, per §2/§5).
3. **Can one static point carry a concept at all?** For *words*, static embeddings were superseded by contextual ones for exactly this reason (polysemy/context-sensitivity) — though distilled static vectors from contextual models are surprisingly strong on lexical tasks (Bommasani et al., ACL 2020) [established], and Backpack sense vectors show a small *set* of static vectors per type can beat a 6B transformer's embeddings on lexical similarity [established]. A kernel of *formal* concepts is single-sense by construction, which blunts the polysemy objection — but the LCM/SONAR experience (§4) shows the analogous failure reappears one level up: a fixed semantic point-space is brittle exactly where content is long, complex, or compositional.

**Bottom line for §6.** Static-in, contextual-after is architecturally routine. The serious form of the objection survives: the model's *semantics* is constructed downstream by trained computation, so the kernel's guarantee attaches to a symbol, not to its interpretation.

## 7. "Knowledge that cannot drift" / hallucination-proof concepts

- No empirical architecture in the literature offers a non-drift or no-hallucination **guarantee**. The closest items: KBLaM's instruction-tuned refusal behaviour ("can learn to refuse when the KB lacks the answer") [established, behavioural, no guarantee]; concept-bottleneck LLMs (arXiv:2412.07992) [claimed] force predictions through human-interpretable concept neurons for classification/controlled generation — interpretability, not immunity; memory-layer architectures (Meta, *Memory Layers at Scale*, arXiv:2412.09764) [established] make factual storage more explicit and editable but still trained and still fallible.
- The one direct "no-hallucination guarantee" claim found — *Transformers are Bayesian Networks* (Coppola, arXiv:2603.17063, single-author preprint, March 2026) [speculative]: a transformer with belief-propagation weights over a fully grounded factor graph "cannot hallucinate". Purely theoretical construction, no experiments, no peer review; assumes away exactly the hard part (full grounding of the knowledge base and BP-conforming weights).
- Model-editing literature (ROME/MEMIT and successors) [established] is a running demonstration that knowledge in LMs is *distributed, interacting, and side-effect-prone*: edits generalise imperfectly and bleed into unrelated facts — the opposite of clean, isolable, drift-proof concept storage.

**Bottom line for §7.** "Cannot drift / cannot hallucinate" currently has zero empirical instances at any scale. Everything that reduces hallucination in practice (RAG, KBLaM, refusal tuning, memory layers) is trained, probabilistic, and partial.

## Verdict against the hypothesis's four claims

**Claim 1 — "The model can never misunderstand kernel concepts" (accuracy): CONTRADICTED.**
The evidence chain is direct: (i) input vectors are already static and models build meaning downstream (detokenization stage, arXiv:2406.19384); (ii) what a model asserts about a concept lives in trained mid-layer MLPs (ROME, arXiv:2202.05262); (iii) models learn essentially as well around frozen *random* or *glyph-pixel* vectors as around semantic ones (arXiv:2507.04886, arXiv:2012.15045), proving the network treats fixed input vectors as arbitrary identifiers whose interpretation it learns itself. A canonical vector pins down a symbol's *identity*, which discrete token IDs already do; it cannot pin down its *interpretation*. Misunderstanding is a property of the trained computation, which the kernel leaves untouched.

**Claim 2 — "Never spends training on kernel concepts" (training efficiency): MOSTLY CONTRADICTED, marginally supported in niches.**
The embedding table is among the least performance-critical parameter blocks (arXiv:2010.12821); freezing it saves little, and *badly matched* frozen vectors cost 5–10× convergence (arXiv:2507.04886). Semantic initialisation of new tokens helps only as a head start that washes out with continued training (arXiv:2407.05841); in-domain frozen init helps only in low-resource regimes (arXiv:2009.14109). The training the kernel would save at the input is repaid by the training the model must spend learning to *use* the imposed geometry — and the concept knowledge itself (in MLPs) still has to be trained.

**Claim 3 — "Dense concept-level I/O beats token-level I/O" (efficiency): TESTED AND CURRENTLY LOSING, with real partial wins.**
Concept-space LMs scale measurably worse than token LMs (α ≈ 0.49–0.57 vs 0.791, arXiv:2508.05305); the frozen SONAR space is fragile for long/complex sentences and sparse as a generation target (arXiv:2412.08821); the tokenizer-free architecture that *does* match token LLMs (BLT, arXiv:2412.09871) got there with learned, dynamic, non-semantic units. Partial wins that are real: decode-step reduction, zero-shot multilinguality (LCM), extreme context compression at 62–73% fidelity (xRAG), linear-scaling KB attention (KBLaM). Dense vector I/O is a useful *lossy compressed channel into trained models*, not a superior native interface.

**Claim 4 — "GNN-style concept I/O and internal rules-based inference": UNTESTED / SPECULATIVE.**
Nothing empirical supports it; the KG-injection literature (ERNIE/KnowBERT era) is the nearest precedent and was brittle and abandoned; the only no-hallucination construction found is an unreviewed theory preprint (arXiv:2603.17063). Coconut-style latent inference works on synthetic tasks but with faithfulness concerns (arXiv:2412.06769, arXiv:2512.21711).

**What survives (the defensible weak form).** Cross-model convergence (arXiv:2209.15430, 2405.07987, 2505.12540) plus E-BERT/xRAG/KBLaM support a *weakened* kernel: canonical concept vectors maintained **outside** the model, attached to already-trained models via small learned (near-linear) adapters, used for knowledge injection, interoperability, and control. Every word that distinguishes this from the programme hypothesis matters: *outside*, *trained models*, *learned adapters*. The untrained, built-in, guarantee-bearing form has no supporting evidence, and its two central claims fail for identified mechanistic reasons, not merely for lack of testing.

## The single strongest argument against, at full strength

**The kernel confuses fixing a symbol with fixing its meaning — and the transformer literature shows these are physically different things in the architecture.** Every transformer already gives each token a frozen-at-inference, externally enumerable input vector, and models trained with those vectors frozen to *arbitrary noise* learn language anyway, because the trained layers above construct their own interpretation function over whatever vectors exist; meanwhile the model's actual knowledge of any concept demonstrably resides in mid-layer MLP key-value memories that the kernel does not touch. Therefore an untrained canonical vector can guarantee only what a token ID already guarantees — stable identity — while the thing the hypothesis needs to guarantee (correct interpretation) remains exactly as trained, exactly as fallible, and exactly as prone to hallucination as before. And the one large-scale attempt to force models to natively read and write a fixed semantic vector space (LCM/SONAR) paid for it with a worse scaling exponent and a fragile, sparse generation target, while the same lab's learned non-semantic latent units (BLT) matched token models. The evidence thus pincers the hypothesis from both sides: where vectors are fixed, they carry no meaning the model is bound by; where fixed vectors were made to carry meaning, the models got worse.
