# PART 2 — OVERALL NATURALNESS

**Rating: 3/10**

**Justification:**

These entries read as artificial and overly self-conscious throughout. While individually some sentences are competent, the cumulative effect is of writing that strains to be exhaustively formal and comprehensive in ways that modern lexicography has largely abandoned.

**Structural problems:**

The entries consistently violate the rhythm and restraint of professional dictionary prose. Consider Entry 6: "a rounded object employed in games and sports, typically struck, kicked, or thrown; it may be hollow or solid, and its size and weight are fixed by the game in which it is used, from the small dimpled kind driven in golf to the large inflated kind kicked in football" — The progression here is pedantic, beginning with abstract formal language ("employed") before descending into concrete examples in a way that feels like exhaustive catalog-making rather than definition.

Entry 1 exemplifies the problem: "feeling fear; troubled by the expectation of harm, danger, or some other unwelcome thing, whether the threat be real or only imagined; unwilling or reluctant to face what is dreaded" — The semicolon-separated clauses accumulate redundantly. "Troubled by the expectation" and "unwilling or reluctant to face what is dreaded" repeat the same semantic content in different registers.

**Diction problems:**

The vocabulary choices are often stilted. Entry 49 uses "herbage" (archaic); Entry 84 uses "fecundity" (elevated register). Entry 94 says "the apparent vault of space arching over the earth" — "vault" and "arching" are self-consciously poetic in ways that professional modern dictionaries avoid. The phrase "natural state of rest that returns each day or night" (Entry 95) is wordy where "a regular period of sleep" would suffice.

**Register inconsistency:**

The entries swing between formal and colloquial without settling into the middle register that characterizes professional lexicography. Entry 29 uses the informal "whatever topics arise as remarks pass back and forth," which sounds conversational rather than lexicographical. Entry 49 drops into the technical term "herbage." Entry 34 uses the pseudo-familiar "kin."

**Comparative assessment:**

The most natural entries are those that are briefest and least elaborated: Entry 32 ("no longer alive; having permanently ceased the functions of life") reads cleanly; Entry 26 (the definition of "cold") is relatively restrained. Entry 31 (definition of "day") manages complexity without excessive parenthetical hedging.

The least natural entries are those that accumulate multiple semicolon-separated clauses and decorative specification: Entry 6 (ball), with its exhaustive catalog of size variations from golf to football, reads like an inventory rather than a definition. Entry 21 (celebrate) adds behavioral narration that exceeds definitional necessity. Entry 37 (event) is vague and circular: "something that happens, especially something of note; an occurrence viewed as a single completed whole, fixed to a particular time and place."

**Overall judgment:**

These read as the work of a conscientious but overwrought writer attempting to be comprehensive and formal rather than clear. A professional lexicographer would trim the redundancies, flatten the register to consistent simplicity, replace the archaic and affected terms, and remove the non-definitional material. The entries aspire to the precision of historical dictionaries but lack the confidence and economy of modern lexicographical practice.
