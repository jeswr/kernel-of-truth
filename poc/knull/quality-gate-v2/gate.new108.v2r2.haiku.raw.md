# PART 2 — OVERALL NATURALNESS

**Rating: 4/10**

**Justification:**

These definitions read as systematically over-elaborated and unnaturally formal in ways that distinguish them sharply from professionally edited dictionary entries. A modern general dictionary—Merriam-Webster, Oxford, or Collins—aims for clarity and economy; these entries are verbose, employ ornate constructions, and flatten linguistic register in ways that feel artificial and archaic.

**Key problems:**

The most unnatural feature is the consistent use of subordinate clauses and semicolons to pile on detail. Entry 4 ("a living organism that feeds on organic matter, is able to move of its own accord, and responds quickly to its surroundings through a nervous system and senses; in ordinary use the word is set against man on the one side and against the plants on the other, and takes in creatures wild and tame alike") uses clotted coordination where a modern dictionary would separate definitional substance from contextual note. The phrase "in ordinary use the word is set against man on the one side and against the plants on the other" is awkwardly phrased; modern dictionaries would say "the term is distinguished from man on one side and plants on the other" or omit the usage note entirely.

Entry 10 ("a warm-blooded egg-laying animal set apart by its feathers, its wings, and its horny beak; most kinds are able to fly, though some are flightless, and all are toothless, the jaws being sheathed in the beak instead") chains qualifications in a manner that feels more like prose exposition than dictionary definition. The clause "the jaws being sheathed in the beak instead" is a Latinate construction that strains naturalness.

Entry 31 ("the interval of light between one night and the next, during which the sun stands above the horizon; also, the whole period of twenty-four hours in which the earth turns once upon its axis, reckoned in ordinary use from one midnight to the next") attempts to reconcile two senses of "day" but does so with ornamental phrasing ("reckoned in ordinary use") that modern dictionaries would either omit or render more plainly.

Entry 7 uses the phrase "haunts the northern forests" — a Romantic-era word choice that feels distinctly non-contemporary: "inhabits" or "lives in" would be standard.

Entry 34's "gives voice by barking rather than by howling alone" is stilted; a modern dictionary would say simply "barks" and omit the comparison to wolves.

Entry 72 ("an adult male human being; a male person who has passed from boyhood to full age; also, in older or general use, a human being of either sex, one of mankind") employs the archaic phrase "one of mankind" in parenthetical context; contemporary dictionaries do not flag such archaisms as part of the main definition.

Entry 95's "the ease with which it is broken" is a strained passive construction; modern style would be "easily broken" or simply omit the comparison.

**Most natural entries:** 27 (color—straightforward, minimal elaboration), 48 (give—concise, uses plain language), 51 (ground—direct and unadorned), 81 (part—economic and clear), 98 (take—simple, direct)

The cumulative effect is of definitions written in a deliberately formal, Latinate register with subordination patterns that feel more suited to 19th-century encyclopedic prose than to modern lexicography. Professional contemporary dictionaries prize directness and economy; these entries prioritize completeness and elaboration at the expense of naturalness.
