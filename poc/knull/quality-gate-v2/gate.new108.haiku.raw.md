The phrase "kept intact" is imprecise and potentially contradictory. "Intact" suggests complete wholeness, but archived material is not necessarily kept in unmodified form—the word choices misstate the nature of archival storage.

# PART 2 — OVERALL NATURALNESS

**Rating: 3/10**

These entries read as substantially artificial and mannered, departing markedly from the natural register of professionally edited general dictionaries. While individual entries occasionally achieve competence, the collection exhibits systematic faults that suggest non-human authorship.

**Justification:**

The entries are hobbled by pervasive verbosity and over-elaboration. Professional dictionaries are concise; these definitions sprawl. Entry 1 exemplifies the problem: "filled with fear; apprehensive of harm or of some unwelcome event, whether real or imagined; gripped by a feeling that ranges from slight unease to terror." A dictionary would write: "feeling fear; anxious or apprehensive." The excess detail ("whether real or imagined," the emotional range) is busywork padding.

Staged observers appear throughout in ways that real dictionaries avoid. Entry 67 is particularly egregious: "radiance emitted by the sun, a flame, or a lamp, by which the eye perceives form and color" — this defines light by reference to its perceptual effect, as if light's essential property is that eyes perceive by it. Real dictionaries define phenomena objectively. Entry 96 ("signifying pleasure, amusement, or goodwill") makes the same error in reverse, reading an observer's interpretation into the physical definition of a smile.

The vague re-naming after initial definition is persistent. Entry 5 re-refers to archived bookmarks as "an entry so treated," and entry 82 awkwardly reiterates "a portrait, landscape, or photograph" after already defining the thing as an image. These are inept.

Non-definitional material is strewn throughout. Entry 30 interpolates: "in an infant it is the chief means of signalling distress." Entry 34 adds historical gloss: "kept since ancient times as a companion." Entry 49 includes functional consequence: "cropped by grazing animals." Real dictionaries confine themselves to what the word denotes; they do not offer ornamental facts about consequences or uses.

Some entries are genuinely well-formed—entry 72 ("an adult male human being; a male person who has passed from boyhood to full age") is direct and clear, and entry 51 ("the solid surface of the earth") achieves professional plainness. But these are islands in a sea of elaboration and artifice.

The **least natural entry** is **5** (the archival bookmark definition): the entire definition is tangled in vague re-reference and functional consequence, collapsing under its own awkwardness.

The **most natural entries** are **72** and **51**: they state the thing simply, without ornamentation or staged observers.

The corpus reads like the output of a system trained to make definitions "complete" rather than clear—to include every possible detail rather than the essential ones. No human lexicographer reviewing for Merriam-Webster would accept this.
