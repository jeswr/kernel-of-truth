The phrasing muddles the definition with qualification.

# PART 2 — OVERALL NATURALNESS

**SCORE: 3/10**

**Justification:**

These entries read as systematically artificial and at odds with how professional lexicographers write. While individual clauses are grammatically correct, the cumulative effect is one of labored, over-explicit prose that avoids the natural concision and idiomatic phrasing of genuine dictionary work.

**Key problems:**

1. **Excessive elaboration without concision:** Entries sprawl with multiple clauses joined by semicolons and commas in ways that suggest exhaustive explanation rather than definition. Entry 4's "in ordinary use the word is distinguished from man on the one side and from the plants on the other, and comprises the creatures wild and tame alike" would never appear in Merriam-Webster or the OED, which would simply state "a living organism distinct from plants." The piling-on of clarifications ("wild and tame alike," "on the one side and on the other") is characteristic of artificial listing, not natural definition.

2. **Archaic or unidiomatic register:** Phrases like "set apart by" (10), "whenever occasion offers" (57), "the one thing and the other" (78), and "the creatures wild and tame alike" (4) feel museum-like. Professional modern dictionaries avoid such dated locution. Entry 83's use of "pledge may be spoken or written" uses elevated diction ("pledge") where modern dictionaries would use "promise."

3. **Persistent non-definitional material:** At least 18 entries contain contrastive clauses, narrated consequences, or decorative flourishes that real lexicographers would excise. Entry 30's entire distinction between crying and watering of the eye is annotation, not definition. Entry 95's comparative clause about how sleep "differs from mere rest" and "from a swoon or a coma" is the kind of over-explanation that marks amateur work. Entry 7 calling a bear's behavior "haunts" is pure literary affect.

4. **Vague anaphoric reference:** Multiple entries re-refer to the defined concept by vague noun phrases ("the creatures," "the item," "such a thing") where a pronoun or zero anaphora would be natural. This is pervasive enough to be a signature fault of these definitions.

5. **Staged observers and unnecessary context:** Entry 94 ("blue by day and dark by night") and Entry 67 ("acts upon the eye to make things visible") insert observers into definitions that should be objective. Entry 82's "sets its subject before the eye when the subject itself is not there" is pure staged observation.

**Least natural entries:** Entry 7 (bear), Entry 4 (animal), Entry 30 (cry), and Entry 95 (sleep) are the worst. Entry 7's "haunts the northern forests" is gratuitously literary. Entry 4's treatment of the animal-versus-plant distinction via "distinguished from man on the one side and from the plants on the other, and comprises the creatures wild and tame alike" reads like amateur philosophical writing, not lexicography. Entry 30's elaborate distinction between crying and eye-watering is pedantic annotation. Entry 95's triple comparative ("differs from mere rest...from a swoon or a coma...easily broken") is characteristic of a student trying to be thorough rather than a professional distilling to essentials.

**Most natural entries:** Entry 31 (day), Entry 42 (fish), Entry 51 (ground), Entry 68 (lose), and Entry 106 (water) approach competence. These are straightforward, lack excessive elaboration, and avoid the worst anaphoric vagueness. Entry 42's "a cold-blooded animal that lives wholly in water, breathing by gills and swimming by means of fins" is clean and direct, though even here "swimming by means of fins" is slightly formal. Entry 51's "the soil or firm land underfoot, on which things rest and over which one walks" would be acceptable in a real dictionary, though the "on which" clauses edge toward the staged-observer problem.

**Overall:** These read unmistakably as generated or artificially composed. They lack the secure economy of professional lexicography—the sense that every word has been weighed and unnecessary material cut. A real dictionary entry on "bear" would not describe its behavior as "haunting" regions. A real entry on "animal" would not laboriously distinguish it from "man" and "plants" via the vague noun phrase "the creatures." The persistent padding with non-essential comparative and contrastive clauses, the archaic register, and the anaphoric vagueness are artifacts of composition that aims at completeness rather than clarity—the opposite of how skilled lexicographers work.
