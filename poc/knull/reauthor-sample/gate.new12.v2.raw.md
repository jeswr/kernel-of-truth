### (a) Overall naturalness: 4/10

The entries are grammatical and generally dictionary-like, but as a set they feel mechanically generated or systematically expanded from shorter definitions. They repeatedly follow the same architecture:

> basic definition; supplementary explanation, contrast, or extension

or:

> basic definition. Applied/Said/Also applied to...

A professional general dictionary would normally show more structural variety: short definitions, numbered senses, usage labels, parenthetical restrictions, examples, and carefully separated literal and figurative senses. Here, nearly every simple word receives a miniature two-part essay.

Some entries are individually convincing—especially 8 and 10—but the collection’s uniform rhythm is conspicuous. Several also contain syntactically loose appendages or redundant restatements that an editor would probably tighten.

### (b) Structural tells

1. Core definition followed by a semicolon and a predictable elaboration

This is the dominant template. Eight entries use a semicolon to divide an initial definition from a second clause:

> “a rounded object used in games and sports ...; hollow or solid, and sized to suit the game”

> “to perform or undergo the first part of an action; to come into being or operation”

> “a stored record of the address ...; in most browsers, an entry in a list”

> “an embrace in which the arms are clasped ...; by extension, any close encircling hold”

> “to bring into existence by shaping ...; to produce or fashion by deliberate work”

> “capable of being seen; perceptible by the eye”

Semicolons are normal in dictionaries, but this frequency and consistency create a generated cadence: define, pause, then paraphrase or elaborate.

2. The second clause often merely restates the first

Several entries use parallel near-synonyms rather than establishing a genuinely separate sense:

> “to bring into existence by shaping, constructing, or combining materials; **to produce or fashion by deliberate work**”

> “capable of being seen; **perceptible by the eye**”

> “to perform or undergo the first part of an action; **to come into being or operation**”

Real dictionaries do sometimes coordinate closely related meanings, but they would usually separate distinct senses or make the semantic distinction clearer. Here, the paired infinitives resemble automatic definitional expansion.

3. Recurrent post-definition qualification by contrast

Entries repeatedly define something and then delimit it by contrasting it with neighboring concepts:

> “distinguished from mere rest by the loss of awareness, and from unconsciousness of other kinds by the ease with which the state is broken”

> “as distinguished from a girl or from a man”

> “rather than search for it anew”

> “rather than of persons”

Again, each construction is possible, but their accumulation suggests a template: core category followed by exclusion or contrast.

4. Detached second sentences beginning with usage-oriented fragments

Four entries switch from the definition to an editorial-sounding afterthought:

> “**Applied alike to** persons, animals, and plants”

> “**Also applied to** a male animal in respect of its offspring”

> “**In general use the word implies** full growth and the capacity to bear children”

> “**Said of** acts, choices, and dealings rather than of persons”

“Applied to” and “said of” are traditional lexicographic formulas, but using them repeatedly in this small set feels imitative, particularly because they appear as detached sentences rather than as labels, sense divisions, or integrated restrictions.

5. Syntactically unanchored material after semicolons

Some continuations do not attach cleanly to the opening definition:

> “a rounded object used in games and sports ...; **hollow or solid, and sized to suit the game**”

After the semicolon, “hollow or solid” has no explicit subject or finite verb. A compact dictionary might tolerate ellipsis, but here it feels like a generated attribute list appended to a complete definition.

Similarly:

> “a domesticated carnivorous mammal ...; **occurs in a great variety of breeds**”

The implied subject is the missing headword, not the grammatical subject of the visible definition. This can occur in dictionaries, but it contributes to the stitched-together quality.

6. Repeated three-part enumerations

The prose favors conspicuously balanced lists:

> “thrown, kicked, or struck”

> “a companion, a guard, or a working animal”

> “affection, comfort, or greeting”

> “shaping, constructing, or combining materials”

> “acts, choices, and dealings”

Such triples are individually natural. At this density, however, they sound like a model repeatedly optimizing definitions into polished three-item lists.

7. Over-elaboration of very simple words

The definitions repeatedly add a second sentence or clause after the word is already adequately defined:

> “no longer alive; having permanently ceased all vital functions. Applied alike to persons, animals, and plants”

> “an adult female human being, as distinguished from a girl or from a man. In general use the word implies full growth and the capacity to bear children”

This produces an unusually uniform “definition plus encyclopedic qualification” structure across unrelated headwords.

### (c) Worst and best

The least natural is 1:

> “a rounded object used in games and sports, especially one that is thrown, kicked, or struck with the hand, the foot, or an implement such as a bat or racket; hollow or solid, and sized to suit the game for which it is intended”

The semicolon introduces a verbless, weakly attached attribute list. “With the hand, the foot, or an implement” is laboriously exhaustive, while “sized to suit the game for which it is intended” sounds like explanatory padding. The whole entry has the feel of successive attributes being appended by a template.

Entry 3 is also strongly templated:

> “a stored record of the address of a document or web page, saved under a short descriptive title so that the reader may return to the material directly rather than search for it anew; in most browsers, an entry in a list kept for the purpose”

The phrase after the semicolon is an incomplete restatement rather than a clean new sense. “Kept for the purpose” is vague and circular; “the reader” is also less natural here than “the user.” Structurally, it reads as a definition followed by an automatically generated implementation note.

Entry 2 has conspicuous padding:

> “to perform or undergo the first part of an action; to come into being or operation, as an event or state does at its outset”

“As an event or state does at its outset” is an awkward explanatory tail that adds little beyond “begin.” It sounds constructed to fill out a parallel second clause.

The most natural is 10:

> “capable of being seen; perceptible by the eye, whether directly or with the aid of an instrument”

It is concise, well attached, and properly narrows the meaning without turning into a mini-essay. The two parts are complementary rather than obviously stitched together.

Entry 8 is also comparatively natural:

> “to bring into existence by shaping, constructing, or combining materials; to produce or fashion by deliberate work”

Its structure is conventional dictionary prose, though the second clause remains somewhat redundant. Taken alone, it could plausibly appear in a published dictionary.

### (d) Discriminability

Yes—on the set as a whole, a fluent reader could probably distinguish it from a real published dictionary with roughly **80–90% accuracy** using structure alone. Classification of individual entries would be much less reliable, perhaps around **60–70%**, because entries such as 8 and 10 are quite plausible in isolation.

The strongest cue would be the repeated two-stage rhythm:

> core definition **;** paraphrase/elaboration

followed in several cases by:

> “Applied...,” “Also applied...,” “In general use...,” or “Said of...”

A real dictionary certainly uses all these devices, but it would be unlikely to deploy them with this regularity across twelve unrelated common words. The decisive tell is not any single semicolon or fragment; it is the unusually uniform pattern of giving every headword a core gloss, a polished supplementary clause, and often a final contrast or usage restriction.
