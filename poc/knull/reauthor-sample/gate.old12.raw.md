### (a) Overall naturalness: 2/10

These do not read like professionally edited general-dictionary entries. Many begin with a plausible defining phrase—“a male parent,” “no longer living,” “able to be seen”—but then expand into repetitive explanatory mini-essays.

The clearest problem is the uniform architecture:

> short definition; additional explanation; consequence or human reaction; final observation

For example:

> “a manufactured plaything with a uniformly round shape; struck or thrown by hand, it rolls or bounces across the ground for a while; children spend hours playing games with one because the activity is enjoyable; its evenness distinguishes it from most other objects”

A general dictionary would almost certainly define this much more concisely. It would not normally explain that children play with it “because the activity is enjoyable” or conclude with a vague discriminating claim about “most other objects.”

The occasional dictionary-like opening prevents a score of 1, but the set as a whole is unmistakably unedited and formula-generated.

### (b) Structural tells

1. **Repeated semicolon-chained architecture**

Almost every entry consists of a short opening definition followed by two or three semicolon-separated expansions:

> “a saved pointer that records the location of a document or page; created by a user so that the material can be reopened quickly; it carries a short descriptive title and spares the reader from searching again”

> “no longer living; having permanently ceased all vital functions; incapable of any further action or awareness”

> “the nightly state of rest in which the body lies still and awareness fades; a slumbering person does not see, barely hears, and knows nothing of events nearby; everyone requires the restorative pause …; dreams may visit…”

Semicolons occur in real dictionaries, but generally to separate senses, closely related subsenses, or formally parallel alternatives. Here they function mechanically as sentence extenders.

2. **Opening noun phrase followed by a reduced passive or participial clause**

Several entries use essentially the same second-clause construction:

> “a manufactured plaything …; **struck or thrown by hand**, it rolls…”

> “a saved pointer …; **created by a user** so that…”

> “an embrace …; **given to express warm affection**…”

> “no longer living; **having permanently ceased**…”

These participial appendages are grammatical or nearly grammatical, but their recurrence makes the entries sound assembled from slots.

3. **A generic consequence clause appended after the definition**

After identifying the object or action, the entries repeatedly explain what it does for people or how people react:

> “**children spend hours playing games with one because the activity is enjoyable**”

> “**it carries a short descriptive title and spares the reader from searching again**”

> “**the gesture comforts and pleases the person receiving it**”

> “**people condemn those who choose to do it**”

This is not merely verbose; it is a recurring structural stage: definition → use → consequence.

4. **Anaphoric placeholders replacing precise lexicographic formulation**

The later clauses repeatedly refer back through vague placeholders:

> “playing games with **one**”

> “**such a parent** commonly shares the home…”

> “**the gesture** comforts and pleases…”

> “**an action of this sort** causes harm…”

> “anyone who looks in the right direction perceives **it**”

This gives the prose the feel of an elementary explanatory paragraph rather than a compact entry. The recurrent frames “such a…,” “the person receiving it,” and “an action of this sort” are particularly conspicuous.

5. **Restatement rather than controlled differentiation**

Several clauses merely paraphrase the opening:

> “no longer living; having permanently ceased all vital functions”

> “to create or produce an object; through deliberate work something is brought into existence that was absent before”

> “an adult female human being; a grown person, no longer a girl, who has reached maturity”

Real definitions sometimes restate for clarity, but these repeatedly inflate a simple definition into multiple near-equivalent clauses without marking distinct senses or nuances.

6. **Category or construction shifts across semicolons**

Entry 2 begins as a verb definition and abruptly changes into a noun phrase:

> “**to start occurring**; **the point at which** a process or activity commences; prior to that point…”

That is structurally unlike a polished dictionary entry. It appears to combine a verb sense and a noun-like explanation without labeling or grammatical separation.

Entry 12 has a similar awkward transition:

> “**morally improper to perform**; **an action of this sort** causes harm…”

The headword is first treated as a predicate applying to an action, then implicitly converted into the action itself.

7. **Recurring “previously absent” frame**

Two unrelated verb entries use almost identical conceptual and syntactic closure:

> “prior to that point **the activity was absent**”

> “something is brought into existence **that was absent before**”

That repeated absence-before/event-after template is a strong sign of systematic generation.

8. **Final moral, emotional, or narrative coda**

Several definitions end with a loosely attached concluding flourish:

> “**its evenness distinguishes it from most other objects**”

> “**breeds vary greatly in size**”

> “**strengthening the bond between the two**”

> “**dreams may visit during the deepest stretches, vanishing by morning**”

These endings do not have a consistent lexicographic function. They resemble a generator adding one more associated fact to complete a paragraph.

### (c) Worst and best

The least natural is entry 1:

> “a manufactured plaything with a uniformly round shape; struck or thrown by hand, it rolls or bounces across the ground for a while; children spend hours playing games with one because the activity is enjoyable; its evenness distinguishes it from most other objects”

It is conspicuously overengineered. “Manufactured plaything” is followed by a usage scene, an unnecessary explanation of why children play, and the almost meaningless claim that roundness distinguishes it from “most other objects.” Structurally, it displays the entire template in its most obvious form.

Entry 9 is nearly as artificial:

> “the nightly state of rest in which the body lies still and awareness fades; a slumbering person does not see, barely hears, and knows nothing of events nearby; everyone requires the restorative pause, and drowsiness demands it after long exertion; dreams may visit during the deepest stretches, vanishing by morning”

This moves from definition to bodily description, then universal biological commentary, then a literary closing image. “Dreams may visit … vanishing by morning” sounds like generated descriptive prose, not lexicography.

Entry 5 is also strongly templated because it becomes a miniature profile:

> “it barks loudly when anything stirs nearby, alerting owners at a distance; renowned for loyalty, it forms warm bonds with its keepers, and most owners return the affection; breeds vary greatly in size”

The most natural is entry 4:

> “no longer living; having permanently ceased all vital functions; incapable of any further action or awareness”

It is concise, grammatically consistent, and its clauses remain definitional. The three-part parallelism is still somewhat mechanical, but this could plausibly appear in a simplified dictionary.

Entry 10 is the next most natural:

> “able to be seen; open to view, so that anyone who looks in the right direction perceives it”

Its first two phrases are dictionary-like. The final “anyone who looks in the right direction perceives it” is unnecessarily literal, but the entry is not structurally sprawling.

### (d) Discriminability

Yes. On sentence structure alone, a fluent reader could probably classify the set correctly with roughly **90–95% accuracy**, especially when shown several entries together.

The strongest cue would be the repeated punctuation-and-clause rhythm:

> compact opening; semicolon; participial or explanatory expansion; semicolon; generic consequence; optional concluding coda

A single item such as entry 4 might pass. But across all twelve, the repeated semicolon scaffolding, vague anaphoric subjects, consequence clauses, and final associated-fact codas would be hard to mistake for the varied, economical structure of a professionally edited dictionary.
