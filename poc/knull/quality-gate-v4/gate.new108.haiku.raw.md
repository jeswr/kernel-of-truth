Entry 4: "any living creature, other than a plant, that feeds on organic matter and moves under its own power" — "organic matter" is imprecise and somewhat technical for a general dictionary; animals feed on food, sustenance, or nourishment, not the technical term "organic matter."

Entry 7: "a massive shaggy-coated mammal with a short tail and powerful limbs" — "shaggy-coated" is awkward; "shaggy" or "shaggy-haired" would be more natural.

Entry 23: "a young person below the age of puberty, or the son or daughter of any parent" — the phrasing is stilted; "the son or daughter of any parent" is an unnatural way to define the familial sense and would be better rendered as "a son or daughter."

Entry 31: "the interval of daylight between one sunrise and the following sunset, or a full round of twenty-four hours" — "a full round of twenty-four hours" is awkward and inelegant; "a period of twenty-four hours" would be more natural.

# PART 2 — OVERALL NATURALNESS

**Rating: 3/10**

These entries read as systematically artificial, betraying their origin as definitions stripped of their headwords and then re-assembled with conspicuous awkwardness. While individual phrases might appear in genuine dictionaries, the cumulative effect is deeply unnatural.

**Justification:**

The most pervasive problem is **syntactic over-formality masquerading as precision**. Entry 71 exemplifies this: "the person by whom a thing was made or created" deploys passive-voice phrasing that no modern general dictionary would employ; compare Oxford's actual entry (the active "one who makes or creates something"). Entry 23—"the son or daughter of any parent"—achieves almost parodic stiltedness in defining "child." Entry 31 piles unnaturalness upon itself: "a full round of twenty-four hours" sounds like bureaucratic jargon, not lexicography.

A second systematic fault is **vague or redundant re-reference** where a natural entry would simply move forward. Entry 37 ("an occurrence or incident, above all one of some note") re-nominates the thing it just defined as the vague "one"; a real dictionary would write "an important occurrence or incident" or simply restructure.

**Staged observers** appear with unnatural frequency. Entry 67 ("radiant energy of the kind the eye perceives") invents a perceiver to assert visibility; a real dictionary would say "radiant energy that makes things visible" or simply describe wavelength. Entry 27 ("the quality...by which the eye tells one hue from another") similarly performs an unnecessary staging.

**Non-definitional flourishes** abound. Entry 50 ("mourning over a loss, in particular over a death") crosses into commentary on emotion rather than denotation. Entry 21 ("festivity...with rejoicing and often ceremony") pads the definition with typical accompaniments. Entry 11 ("at which independent life commences") imports philosophical assertion about personhood.

**Most natural entries** (though still wooden): 1, 2, 6, 9, 18, 26, 31, 35, 39, 42, 44, 52, 55, 72, 85, 90, 94. These tend to be concrete, simple, and avoid the worst excesses—though even entry 26 ("of low temperature, notably lacking in warmth or heat") contains the redundancy "lacking in warmth or heat."

**Least natural entries**: 23 ("the son or daughter of any parent"), 71 ("the person by whom a thing was made or created"), 31 ("a full round of twenty-four hours"), 50, and 87 ("a note or token that brings back to mind"). These accumulate multiple faults: register violations, vagueness, and awkward re-reference.

The entries collectively read like they were written by someone who learned English from a grammar manual rather than from reading actual dictionaries. A Merriam-Webster or Oxford entry on the same words would be more direct, more economical, and far less theatrically formal.
