## Part 1 — Defect audit

### 1. Register or word-choice faults

- **29:** “**an informal exchange of speech**” — *exchange of speech* is not a natural English collocation. *Exchange of words*, *spoken exchange*, or *informal conversation* would be idiomatic.

- **62:** “**fixing how it is counted and reidentified**” — this is abstruse philosophical jargon, and *how it is counted* is especially unnatural here. It is out of register for a general definition of *kind*.

- **71:** “**the person whose work has brought a thing into being**” — an affected circumlocution for *a person who makes or creates something*. The wording sounds manufactured rather than lexicographically economical.

- **80:** “**give entry or view**” — both collocations are defective. A door or cover may *allow entry*, *give access*, or *afford a view*; it does not ordinarily “give entry or view.”

## Part 2 — Overall naturalness

**8/10.**

Most entries are concise, controlled, and convincingly dictionary-like. Strong examples include:

- **34:** “**a domesticated carnivorous mammal of the wolf family, kept as a companion or worker**”
- **43:** “**the blossom of a plant, containing its organs of reproduction**”
- **83:** “**a binding assurance that one will do, or refrain from doing, a stated thing**”
- **97:** “**the star around which the earth revolves and from which it receives light and heat**”

These use conventional genus-and-differentia structure and economical dictionary diction.

The collection nevertheless feels somewhat artificially uniform. Many entries repeatedly use the same polished template—“feeling or showing,” “a person whose,” “to bring,” and semicolon-linked paraphrases. The conspicuously unnatural wording in “**an informal exchange of speech**” (29), “**fixing how it is counted and reidentified**” (62), and “**give entry or view**” (80) breaks the professional illusion.

- **Least natural:** **62**, because its philosophical formulation is opaque and sharply mismatched with the straightforward treatment of the other common words.
- **Most natural:** **34**, which is compact, idiomatic, informative, and indistinguishable from an edited general-dictionary definition.
